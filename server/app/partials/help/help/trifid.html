TRIFID is a machine learning tool that predicts the functional relevance of protein isoforms. Trained on real data from large-scale proteomics experiments, it uses features from the APPRIS database and external inputs to score each annotated isoform. It uses a random forest-based predictor of splice isoform functional importance based on unbiased data from large-scale mass spectrometry, using protein isoforms detected in these proteomics experiments as a proxy for functional significance at the protein level. The higher the score, the more likely the isoform is to be functionally important.
It can distinguish isoforms that are under selective pressure from those that are not. In testing, we have found that isoforms with a score above 0.6 are under selective pressure. These isoforms are marked in bold in the APPRIS database.

Non-synonymous to synonymous (NS/Syn) ratios for rare (yellow) and common (purple) allele frequencies for non-overlapping alternative exons from the human gene set. Exons are binned by their TRIFID score. Exons with scores above 0.6 have significantly lower NS/Syn ratios for common allele frequencies, so are under selective pressure. Exons with scores below 0.6 do not. From Pozo et al. 2021.

Alternative exons that score highly in TRIFID are also much more likely to have pathogenic variants, which reinforces the likelihood that transcripts with these exons are functionally important. Proportionally, TRIFID alternative transcripts with scores above 0.8 harbour 700 times more pathogenic mutations than the the lowest scoring TRIFID alternative transcripts.

The percentage of alternative exons that contain pathogenic mutations in the ClinVar database. Again, exons are binned by TRIFID score. From Pozo et al. 2022.

The most important features in TRIFID are conservation-based, but information from annotators, the effect on protein structure and function, and transcript expression levels also play a part in determining the TRIFID score.

The top 18 features in TRIFID divided into 5 sub-types (evolutionary, annotation, structure/functional, expression and splicing effects). CORSAIR, Matador3D and Spade are all APPRIS-based scores. A lower case “n” indicates that the feature was normalized. From Pozo et al, 2021.

Protein isoforms predicted as functionally important by the algorithm had higher cross-species conservation and significantly fewer broken functional domains. TRIFID has been applied to all species in APPRIS.
